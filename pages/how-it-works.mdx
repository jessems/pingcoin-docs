# How It Works

Understanding the science behind Pingcoin helps you use it effectively and trust its results.

## The Physics of Sound

When you tap a coin, it vibrates. These vibrations create pressure waves in the air — what we hear as sound. The characteristics of this sound depend entirely on the coin's physical properties.

### Resonance Frequencies

Every object has natural frequencies at which it "wants" to vibrate — these are called resonance frequencies. For a coin, these frequencies depend on:

| Factor | Effect on Sound |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Material composition** | Different metals vibrate at different frequencies. Gold, silver, copper, and tungsten all have unique acoustic signatures. |
| **Diameter** | Larger coins have lower base frequencies |
| **Thickness** | Thicker coins have higher frequencies |
| **Mass distribution** | Relief patterns affect how vibrations propagate |

### Why This Defeats Counterfeits

A counterfeiter trying to fake a gold coin faces a fundamental problem: they need to match the weight (so it passes scale tests) and the size (so it looks right). But if they use a cheaper metal, the acoustic signature will be completely different.

**Tungsten-core fakes** are particularly interesting. Tungsten has nearly the same density as gold, so a tungsten-filled coin can pass weight tests perfectly. However, tungsten has very different elastic properties:

- **Gold**: Soft, ductile, rings beautifully with sustained vibration
- **Tungsten**: Hard, brittle, produces a dull "thud" that decays quickly

This acoustic difference is unmistakable to Pingcoin's analysis.

## The Analysis Process

Here's what happens when you test a coin:

### 1. Recording
The app records 2-3 seconds of audio using your phone's microphone after you tap the coin.

### 2. Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)
The raw audio waveform is converted into a frequency spectrum using FFT — a mathematical technique that reveals which frequencies are present and how strong each one is.

### 3. Peak Detection
The algorithm identifies the dominant frequency peaks in the spectrum. Coins typically produce several distinct peaks corresponding to different vibration modes.

### 4. Pattern Matching
These peaks are compared against our database of reference values for the coin type you're testing. The app looks for:
- Correct frequency positions (within tolerance)
- Appropriate peak ratios
- Expected decay characteristics

### 5. Verdict
Based on how well the recorded frequencies match the expected pattern, the app returns:
- ✅ **Pass** — Frequencies match authentic coin profile
- ❌ **Fail** — Significant frequency mismatch detected
- ⚠️ **Inconclusive** — Unable to make a confident determination

## Understanding Results

### A Pass Means...

Both the geometry AND material properties of your coin fall within the expected range for authentic coins. This is strong evidence of authenticity.

### A Fail Means...

The coin's acoustic signature differs significantly from authentic examples. This could indicate:
- Counterfeit coin (wrong material)
- Wrong coin type selected
- Damaged or altered coin
- Environmental interference during testing

### Inconclusive Results

Sometimes the app can't make a confident call. This might happen due to:
- Background noise
- Coin not ringing cleanly (poor tap technique)
- Unusual but legitimate coin variant
- Microphone limitations

See our [Testing Guide](/testing-guide) for tips on getting clean results.

## Frequency Examples

Here are typical resonance frequencies for popular coins:

| Coin | Primary Frequency | Ring Duration |
|------|-------------------|---------------|
| American Silver Eagle (1 oz) | ~6,145 Hz | 8-12 seconds |
| Canadian Silver Maple Leaf | ~6,200 Hz | 4-6 seconds* |
| Gold Krugerrand (1 oz) | ~4,523 Hz | 10-15 seconds |
| Gold American Eagle (1 oz) | ~4,100 Hz | 8-12 seconds |

*The Silver Maple Leaf's shorter ring is due to its .9999 fine silver composition — see [False Positives](/false-positives) for details.

## Limitations

No testing method is perfect. Acoustic testing has some limitations:

1. **Requires a clean tap** — Poor technique can produce unclear results
2. **Ambient noise sensitive** — Test in a quiet environment
3. **Phone microphone quality varies** — Results may differ between devices
4. **Some edge cases exist** — See [False Positives](/false-positives)

For high-value purchases, we recommend using Pingcoin alongside other verification methods like weight/dimension checks and visual inspection.

## The Technology Stack

For the technically curious, Pingcoin uses:

- **TarsosDSP** — Real-time audio processing library for pitch detection and FFT
- **FFTpack** — Efficient Fast Fourier Transform implementation
- **Firebase** — User authentication and cloud storage
- **MPAndroidChart** — Frequency spectrum visualization

The core algorithm is open source, so you can review exactly how your coins are being analyzed.
